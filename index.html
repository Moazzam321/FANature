Overview of Nature

“I declare this world is so beautiful that I can hardly believe it exists.” The beauty of nature can have a profound effect upon our senses, those gateways from the outer world to the inner, whether it results in disbelief in its very existence as Emerson notes, or feelings such as awe, wonder, or amazement. But what is it about nature and the entities that make it up that cause us, oftentimes unwillingly, to feel or declare that they are beautiful? One answer that Emerson offers is that “the simple perception of natural forms is a delight.” When we think of beauty in nature, we might most immediately think of things that dazzle the senses – the prominence of a mountain, the expanse of the sea, the unfolding of the life of a flower.

Often it is merely the perception of these things itself which gives us pleasure, and this emotional or affective response on our part seems to be crucial to our experience of beauty. So in a way there is a correlate here to the intrinsic value of nature; Emerson says:"the sky, the mountain, the tree, the animal, give us a delight in and for themselves". The close observer of nature sees a river in constant flux, even when the river’s water is frozen and everything appears to be static and unchanging for a time. Nature can reveal its beauty in all places and at all times to the eye that knows how to look for it. an emotional or affective component in the beauty of the intellect just as there is in the immediate beauty of perception. If we destroy the natural world, we take away the things that we can marvel at and experience awe towards in these two ways.

What is Climate Change?

Climate change, also called global warming, refers to the rise in average surface temperatures on Earth. An overwhelming scientific consensus maintains that climate change is due primarily to the human use of fossil fuels, which releases carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases into the air. The gases trap heat within the atmosphere, which can have a range of effects on ecosystems, including rising sea levels, severe weather events, and droughts that render landscapes more susceptible to wildfires. There is broad-based agreement within the scientific community that climate change is real. The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration concur that climate change is indeed occurring and is almost certainly due to human activity.

The primary cause of climate change is the burning of fossil fuels, such as oil and coal, which emits greenhouse gases into the atmosphere—primarily carbon dioxide. Other human activities, such as agriculture and deforestation, also contribute to the proliferation of greenhouse gases that cause climate change. While some quantities of these gases are a naturally occurring and critical part of Earth’s temperature control system, the atmospheric concentration of CO2 did not rise above 300 parts per million between the advent of human civilization roughly 10,000 years ago and 1900. Today it is at about 400 ppm, a level not reached in more than 400,000 years. While consensus among nearly all scientists, scientific organizations, and governments is that climate change is happening and is caused by human activity, a small minority of voices questions the validity of such assertions and prefers to cast doubt on the preponderance of evidence.

Rising temperatures

Because human-induced warming is superimposed on a naturally varying climate, the temperature rise has not been, and will not be, uniform or smooth across the country or over time. Learn More

Sea-Level Rise

Global sea level has risen by about 8 inches since reliable record keeping began in 1880. It is projected to rise another 1 to 4 feet by 2100. This is the result of added water from melting land ice and the expansion of seawater as it warms. Learn More

Solutions for Climate Change

When we talk about solutions for climate change, the main goal that we dispute about is the reduction of carbon in the environment, as carbon is the factor which causes retention of heat in the atmosphere resulting in accelerated climate change. The common person might propose the idea of making depot spaces for carbon to store the excess carbon. This might sound like a good idea to revolve our solutions around, but what we do not realize is that ecosystems and plants act as natural repositories for carbon. As Turner et al. say, “forests, peatlands, oceans, and other ecosystems control carbon and other global biogeochemical cycles. The oceans alone sequester about 2 gigatonnes of carbon a year.“ This large amount of carbon indicates the importance of ecosystems in recycling and maintaining the carbon levels of our environment. When we talk about practicality, environmental conservatives assert to trim the passenger vehicle emissions around the globe, but reducing the rates of deforestation can hold the same effect that removing emissions from passenger vehicles worldwide would have had. In fact, it is estimated that minimizing deforestation and forest degradation rates would bring down global emissions by up to one gigaton of carbon a year, which is more than the emissions of all passenger cars combined. (Turner et al.) We need to discern the two-way relationship between the natural world and climate: we need to recognize how nature helps to regulate the climate and save us from the disastrous effects that it could have otherwise. As Foley et al. say, "While it may be obvious that the climate affects terrestrial ecosystems, terrestrial ecosystems can also affect the climate, particularly through their vegetative cover and soil.” (38) Now apart from reducing the rates of deforestation, restoring lost vegetation can also have a surprisingly good effect on the rates of climate change. The restoration of the world's marginal and degraded lands to natural habitats could consume an additional 0.65 gigaton annually, helping to control global warming by slacking off the environment's carbon presence. (Turner et al.)

The burning of fossil fuels generates a large number of global warming emissions which is one of the main reasons for the worsening climate situation today. The alternatives for fossil fuels lie in the use of renewable sources, such as wind, biomass, solar power, and tidal power to produce energy. Now, if we take the instance of the United States we know that “In the United States, about 29 percent of global warming emissions come from our electricity sector. Most of those emissions come from fossil fuels like coal and natural gas” (Union Of Concerned Scientists, “Benefits of Renewable Energy Use.”) These numbers are a clear measure of how renewable energy sources, which are free of global warming emissions, can turn out to be a game changer in our battle with climate change as they can be a perfect replacement for global warming emissions caused by non-renewable resources. When we compare and contrast the emissions given out by renewable sources and non-renewable sources, the differences are quite clear. The burning of natural gas for generating electricity produces between 0.6 and 2 pounds of carbon dioxide equivalent per kilowatt-hour (CO2E/kWh); coal is responsible for 1.4 to 3.6 pounds of CO2E/kWh while wind, on the other hand, is the factor for 0.02 to 0.04 pounds of CO2E/kWh on a life-cycle basis. Solar generates 0.07 to 0.2 pound, geothermal 0.1 and 0.2 pounds, and hydroelectric between 0.1 and 0.5 pounds which clearly manifest how cleaner renewable energy can be against non-renewable sources in contrast to their carbon production(Union Of Concerned Scientists, “Benefits of Renewable Energy Use.”). An analysis done by the Union of Concerned Scientists in 2009 found that a 25 percent by 2025 national renewable electricity standard would lower power plant CO2 emissions 277 million metric tons annually by 2025—the equivalent of the annual output from 70 typical (600 MW) new coal plants, which is a considerable amount (“Benefits of Renewable Energy Use.”)
